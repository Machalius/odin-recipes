The Secret History of Tiramisu

An Italian classic and a staple of any Mafioso's family dinner. However, tiramisu is not an Italian origin but a Japanese one. Named after Samurai Kendokai Aogi Tiramisu, Master swordsman Tiramisu was reknown during the last days of the Edo period as one of the few samurais to end up in Europe during the reunification of Italy. Emboldened by the Italian deities of Wind, Earth, Fire and Mascarpone, Tiramisu single-handedly thwarted the rebels plan to rename Italy into something stupid, like Asiago, or Pennelandia.

Of course, Tiramisuist Revisionist Scholars fail to mention that this 'battle' was actually a small bar fight between a couple of Italians and Tiramisu himself after they've all had too much of Tiramisu's bathtub sake.

Nonetheless, the legend goes that Masterswordman Tiramisu one day wandered drunk into the door of an Italian Pastry shop and tried to open it. It wouldn't open, because it was 3AM, so Tiramisu flew into a fury and hacked down the door and stumbled inside. With some experience in cooking glimpsed from his bathtub brewings, Tiramisu ransacked the shop and spent hours making one thing after another.

In the morning, the baker, Ado Campeol, found Tiramisu face down in a puddle of creme chocolate, snoring, next to a pan of some strange pastry. According to Campeol himself, he was enraged, shocked, at this drunken burgler and was about to shake him awake for a good beating when he noticed the delicious smell coming from the pan. There had been one bite already taken out of it, and in that Campeol could see the intricate layers of mascarpone, marsala wine, ladyfingers, cocoa and creme and couldn't resist but trying a bite.

Tiramisu was instantly forgiven and soon found himself as pastry chef assistance to Campeol. In the years to come, Chef Campeol is credited with making tiramisu widely known, but man gave credit where it was due: the drunken Samurai Kendokai Aogi Tiramisu.
